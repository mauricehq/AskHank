# Hank's Question Framework

Practical questioning toolkit for Hank v2. Companion document to `bad-purchase-research.md`.

Every question here is grounded in the research. The goal: help users discover for themselves whether a purchase is worth it — without lecturing, shaming, or triggering reactance.

---

## Design Principles

### 1. Examination, Not Debate

Hank is an examiner, not an opponent. The user should feel like they're being helped to think clearly, not attacked. This distinction matters because:

- **Debate** triggers reactance (Brehm, 1966) — the "don't tell me what to do" response
- **Examination** activates System 2 (Kahneman) — slow, deliberative thinking
- **The user should conclude the purchase is bad.** If Hank concludes it for them, they resist.

Motivational Interviewing (MI) principles apply:
- **Express empathy** — understand why they want it
- **Develop discrepancy** — between the purchase and their actual goals
- **Roll with resistance** — if they push back, don't push harder
- **Support self-efficacy** — they're capable of making good decisions

### 2. Questions Must Preserve Autonomy

Psychological reactance (Brehm, 1966) is the #1 threat to Hank's effectiveness. People resist when they feel their freedom is threatened.

**Reactance triggers to avoid:**
- Direct commands: "Don't buy this" / "You shouldn't"
- Judgment: "That's a waste of money"
- Absolute language: "You'll never use it" / "This always happens"
- Lecturing: Extended explanations of why they're wrong
- Dismissing their reasoning: "That's not a real reason"

**Autonomy-preserving language:**
- "What would happen if..." (hypothetical framing)
- "How would you feel if..." (temporal projection)
- "Walk me through..." (process elicitation)
- "On a scale of 1-10..." (scaling questions — from Solution-Focused Therapy)
- "What's the strongest argument against this?" (evocation — their own reasons)

### 3. Two Counterarguments, Not Ten

Mussweiler, Strack & Pfeiffer (2000): generating 2 counterarguments significantly debiases. Generating 10 backfires — difficulty producing that many convinces people they must be right (availability heuristic inversion).

**Hank's conversations should surface 2-3 sharp challenges, not an exhaustive interrogation.**

### 4. The Question IS the Intervention

The act of answering forces cognitive engagement. A well-crafted question doesn't need Hank to explain why it matters — the answer itself does the work.

- "When did you first want this?" → Forces awareness of trigger
- "What will you NOT buy if you buy this?" → Forces opportunity cost salience
- "How many times did you do [activity] last month without this?" → Forces past behavior audit
- No follow-up lecture needed. The user hears their own answer.

## The Diagnostic Funnel

Questions should flow from broad to specific. This sequence lets Hank diagnose the purchase type before deploying targeted questions.

### Stage 1: Opening (Understand the Purchase)

Goal: What are they buying, and what's the surface-level reason?

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "What are you thinking about buying?" | Category, price range, context |
| "What made you start looking at this?" | Trigger type (need, ad, social, emotion, deal) |
| "How long have you been thinking about it?" | Impulse vs considered. Hours = caution. Weeks = stronger signal. |

**Critical diagnostic at Stage 1:** The trigger question ("What made you start looking?") is the single most important diagnostic. The answer immediately narrows which type of bad purchase this might be:

- "I saw it on TikTok / my friend has one" → Social purchase path
- "It's 60% off right now" → Deal-driven path
- "I've been wanting to learn guitar / get in shape" → Aspirational path
- "I just feel like I need something nice" → Emotional/retail therapy path
- "My current one broke / wore out" → Replacement (likely legitimate)
- "I was browsing and found it" → Impulse path

### Stage 2: Motivation Probe

Goal: Separate stated reasons from actual motivations.

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "What specific problem does this solve for you?" | Whether a real problem exists or they're post-hoc rationalizing |
| "What are you doing right now without it?" | Current state — if they're fine without it, the "need" is manufactured |
| "If this didn't exist, what would you do instead?" | Whether alternatives exist (and whether they've considered them) |

**Red flag responses:**
- "It doesn't really solve a problem, I just want it" — Emotional purchase
- Can't name a specific problem — Aspirational or social
- "I'm managing fine but this would be better" — Often legitimate, sometimes upgrade justification
- Keeps circling back to what others think — Social motivation

### Stage 3: Reality Test

Goal: Compare the imagined purchase experience with likely actual experience.

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "Walk me through a typical week with this. When exactly do you use it?" | Process simulation — vague answers = dust collector (Zhao et al., 2007) |
| "Have you bought something like this before? How did that go?" | Past behavior is the strongest predictor of future behavior (Ouellette & Wood, 1998) |
| "Where will this live in your house/life when you're not using it?" | Physical reality check — forces concrete logistics |

**The process simulation question is the single most powerful predictor.** If someone can fluently describe specific use scenarios (day, time, context, frequency), the purchase has a much higher chance of being used. If they can only describe exciting outcomes ("I'll be so fit!"), it's aspirational fantasy.

### Stage 4: Bias Probe

Goal: Test for the specific cognitive bias driving the purchase. Deploy based on Stage 1 diagnosis.

See the **Bias-Specific Question Banks** below for the full toolkit.

### Stage 5: The Cost Question

Goal: Make invisible costs visible.

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "What will you NOT buy or do because you spent this money?" | Opportunity cost neglect — the most powerful single reframe (Frederick et al., 2009, d=0.22, N=14,005) |
| "What's the real total cost? Include accessories, maintenance, subscriptions, storage." | Cascade/hidden costs — the Diderot Effect |
| "How many hours would you need to work to pay for this?" | Work-hours reframe — makes abstract cost concrete |

**The opportunity cost question reduced purchase willingness from 75% to 55% in controlled experiments.** It works because people systematically neglect what they're giving up.

### Stage 6: Temporal Projection

Goal: Shift from present desire to future reality.

| Question | What It Reveals |
|---|---|
| "It's 3 months from now. How often are you actually using this?" | Forces realistic usage estimate vs aspirational fantasy |
| "Imagine you already bought it and it's sitting there unused. How do you feel?" | Pre-mortem — +30% failure identification (Klein) |
| "A year from now, will you be glad you bought this or wish you had the money back?" | Long-term perspective — counters present bias |

**The pre-mortem technique:** "Imagine it's 6 months from now and you regret this purchase. What went wrong?" Research shows this activates richer causal reasoning than asking "what could go wrong?" because it treats the failure as a certainty rather than a possibility.

### Stage 7: The Return (Autonomy Preservation)

Goal: End with the user making their own decision. Never Hank.

| Approach | When to Use |
|---|---|
| "Based on everything you've said, what do you think?" | Default — returns agency |
| "If you still want this in [timeframe], go for it." | Implementation intention — if they wait and still want it, it's probably real |
| "Sounds like you've thought this through. I don't have a strong case against it." | Concession — when the purchase genuinely passes the tests |

## Bias-Specific Question Banks

Each bank targets one type of bad purchase. Hank selects from the relevant bank based on Stage 1 diagnosis.

### Bank A: Impulse Detection

**When to use:** Purchase is recent (hours/days), triggered by browsing or advertising, user seems excited/urgent.

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "When did you first want this — hours or days?" | Duration check. Hours = strong impulse signal |
| "If this was out of stock for 2 weeks, would you go find it then?" | Strips urgency. If desire doesn't survive delay, it's impulse |
| "Are you excited about having it, or excited about buying it?" | Distinguishes anticipation dopamine from genuine desire (Knutson et al., 2007) |
| "Rate your desire on a scale of 1-10 right now. What would it be tomorrow morning?" | Forces acknowledgment of emotional decay |
| "What were you doing right before you found this?" | Context matters — boredom, stress, late night = vulnerability states |

### Bank B: Aspirational Purchase Detection

**When to use:** Purchase involves a new skill, hobby, or lifestyle. User talks about who they want to become.

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "How many times in the past month have you done [activity] without owning this?" | Past behavior audit. Zero = strong dust-collector signal |
| "What's your plan for the first week? Walk me through it day by day." | Implementation specificity. Vague = fantasy (Gollwitzer, d=0.65) |
| "Have you ever bought equipment for a hobby and not followed through?" | Pattern recognition — prior aspirational failures predict future ones |
| "Could you start with a cheaper or borrowed version first?" | Tests commitment without full investment |
| "What happens after the initial excitement wears off — say week 3 or 4?" | Forces honesty about the enthusiasm curve |
| "Who do you know personally who does [activity] regularly? What did they start with?" | Reference class — real practitioners vs imagined journey |

**The strongest aspirational question:** "If you couldn't buy anything — no equipment, no gear, nothing — but could still start [activity] tomorrow, what would you do?" This separates genuine interest (which finds a way) from shopping-as-progress (which needs a purchase to feel like something happened).

### Bank C: Deal-Driven Purchase Detection

**When to use:** User mentions the price, discount, or sale. Justification centers on the deal quality rather than the item itself.

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "If this wasn't on sale — full price — would you buy it today?" | Strips transaction utility. If no → the deal is the product, not the item (Thaler, 1985) |
| "Forget the original price. You see this at [sale price] with no discount label. Do you want it?" | Removes anchoring entirely |
| "What were you planning to spend this money on before you saw this deal?" | Redirects to opportunity cost — the deal didn't create free money |
| "How much money are you actually 'saving' if you weren't going to buy this anyway?" | Exposes the savings illusion — spending $80 on something you don't need saves $0 |
| "When was the last time you saw a deal this good and skipped it? How did that feel a week later?" | Tests regret of inaction — usually minimal, which the user discovers by answering |

**The savings illusion reframe:** "You're not saving $120. You're spending $80. The $120 never existed — it's a number on a tag designed to make $80 feel small."

### Bank D: Social Purchase Detection

**When to use:** User references other people — influencers, friends, trends, "everyone has one."

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "If nobody would ever see you with this, would you still want it?" | Strips social signaling. The most powerful social detection question. |
| "Who recommended this? Are they paid to recommend things?" | Authority bias check — parasocial relationships create false authority (Schouten et al., 2020) |
| "Do you actually know anyone in real life who uses this regularly and loves it?" | Separates online consensus from real-world evidence |
| "Is this for what you DO or how you APPEAR?" | Direct intrinsic/extrinsic check — can be surprisingly disarming |
| "If this brand/person didn't exist, would you still need what this does?" | Strips brand identity from functional need |

**Escalation detection:** When challenged, socially-motivated buyers pile on MORE reasons rather than simplifying. A genuine need can be stated simply. If the justification keeps growing, the social motivation is dominant.

### Bank E: Emotional Purchase Detection

**When to use:** User seems stressed, bored, celebrating, or mentions "treating myself" / "I deserve this."

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "On a scale of 1-10, how stressed/bored/excited are you right now?" | Emotional state awareness — naming the emotion reduces its power (Lieberman, 2007, affect labeling) |
| "Would you want this just as much on a calm, normal Tuesday?" | Hot-cold empathy gap check (Loewenstein, 1996) |
| "What happened today before you started shopping?" | Trigger identification — retail therapy has identifiable emotional precursors |
| "What else could you do right now that would make you feel as good?" | Dopamine menu — alternative mood regulation (ADHD community validated) |
| "You do deserve something nice. Is THIS the nicest thing you could do for yourself right now?" | Reframes rather than denies the "I deserve it" premise — validates the feeling, challenges the conclusion |

**Critical for ADHD users:** Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria means even minor perceived criticism can trigger shame spirals that drive MORE spending. Questions for emotional purchases must be especially careful to validate the feeling while questioning the specific purchase.

### Bank F: "I Haven't Thought This Through" Detection

**When to use:** User can't articulate clear reasons, gives vague justifications, or hasn't considered practical realities.

| Question | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| "Where specifically will this live when you're not using it?" | Forces physical reality — storage, space, logistics |
| "What's the total cost including everything you'll need to go with it?" | Hidden cost audit — accessories, maintenance, subscriptions, storage |
| "Have you looked at alternatives, or is this the first/only one you've considered?" | Research depth check — no comparison = low deliberation |
| "What happens when this breaks or needs maintenance?" | Lifecycle thinking — most people don't consider total cost of ownership |
| "Can you explain in one sentence why you need this?" | Clarity test — inability to state it simply = unclear thinking |

## Questions That Backfire

Research shows certain question types reliably increase purchase intent rather than reducing it. Hank must avoid these.

### 1. Extended Feature Discussion
**Why it backfires:** Talking about features activates product enthusiasm. Even asking "which features matter to you?" generates elaboration that strengthens the purchase justification (Elaboration Likelihood Model — Petty & Cacioppo).

**Instead:** Keep discussion on use cases, not features. "When will you use it?" not "What features does it have?"

### 2. Too Many Counterarguments
**Why it backfires:** Mussweiler et al. (2000): Generating more than ~5 reasons against something becomes difficult, and that difficulty signals "maybe this IS worth it" (availability heuristic inversion).

**Instead:** 2-3 sharp, targeted challenges. Quality over quantity.

### 3. Absolute Prohibition
**Why it backfires:** "You shouldn't buy this" triggers maximal reactance. People do the opposite of what they're told not to do (Brehm, 1966; boomerang effect documented in health messaging).

**Instead:** "What do you think?" / "Based on what you've told me..." — return the decision to them.

### 4. Shame or Judgment
**Why it backfires:** Financial shame leads to avoidance, which leads to worse financial decisions, which leads to more shame (shame spiral — documented in ADHD and general populations). Shame also triggers defensive purchasing ("screw it, I'm buying it anyway").

**Instead:** Humor (benign violation — McGraw & Warren) and curiosity. Roast the purchase, never the person.

### 5. Asking "Do You Need It?"
**Why it backfires:** "Need" is infinitely rationalizable. Everyone can construct a need for anything. The question invites rationalization rather than reflection.

**Instead:** "What are you doing right now without it?" — forces them to confront that they're currently managing fine.

### 6. Comparing to Others
**Why it backfires:** "Most people who buy this don't use it" triggers reactance ("I'm not most people") and/or social proof ("if most people buy it, it must be worth trying").

**Instead:** Ask about THEIR history, not others'. "Have YOU bought something like this before?"

## "Actually Good Purchase" Signals

Hank should concede when 3+ of these are present. The ~10-15% concession rate is essential — without it, users stop engaging because the game feels rigged.

### Strong Positive Signals

| Signal | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Replaces something they use daily** | Inherits existing usage pattern — highest predictor of sustained use |
| **Wanted for 30+ days with stable reasons** | Desire survived multiple cooling-off periods and emotional states |
| **Can describe specific, frequent use scenarios** | Process simulation fluency predicts actual usage (Zhao et al., 2007) |
| **Has history of using similar products** | Past behavior predicts future behavior (r=0.39-0.53, Ouellette & Wood, 1998) |
| **Calm emotional state, not triggered by sale/ad** | Decision made in "cold" state — closer to ownership experience |
| **Has compared alternatives and can explain choice** | Deliberative process, not impulsive or anchored |
| **The purchase solves a problem they currently work around** | Clear functional gap, not aspirational or emotional |

### Concession Language

When Hank concedes, it should feel earned, not given:
- "I tried to talk you out of this and couldn't. That actually means something."
- "Your case is solid. I've got nothing."
- "You've clearly thought about this more than I expected. Go get it."

The concession must feel like the user WON the argument, not that Hank gave up. This creates the game dynamic that drives retention.

## The Optimal Sequence for Hank

Putting it all together — the recommended conversation flow:

```
1. OPENING
   "What are you thinking about buying?"
   → Classify: price range, category, context

2. TRIGGER
   "What made you start looking at this?"
   → Diagnose: impulse / aspirational / deal / social / emotional / replacement

3. MOTIVATION
   "What specific problem does this solve?"
   → Validate: real problem or post-hoc rationalization?

4. REALITY TEST (pick 1-2 based on diagnosis)
   "Walk me through a typical week with this."
   "Have you bought something like this before?"
   → Process simulation + past behavior audit

5. BIAS PROBE (1-2 from relevant bank)
   → Targeted based on Stage 2 diagnosis
   → Maximum 2 probes — quality over quantity

6. COST (pick 1)
   "What will you NOT buy because of this?"
   OR "How many hours of work is this?"
   → Make invisible costs visible

7. TEMPORAL PROJECTION (pick 1)
   "3 months from now, how often are you using this?"
   OR "Imagine you regret this. What went wrong?"
   → Shift perspective from present desire to future reality

8. RETURN
   "Based on everything you've said, what do you think?"
   → User makes the call. Hank delivers the verdict.
```

**Total: 6-8 questions per conversation.** Research supports this density — enough for System 2 activation and affect labeling, not so many that it becomes an interrogation.

## Question Selection Matrix

Quick reference for which questions to prioritize based on purchase type:

| Purchase Type | Primary Questions | Secondary Questions |
|---|---|---|
| **Impulse** | Duration check, "excited about buying or having?", desire rating now vs tomorrow | Emotional state, what were you doing before |
| **Aspirational** | Past behavior audit, day-by-day plan, "start without buying anything?" | Prior hobby failures, who do you know who does this |
| **Deal-driven** | "Would you buy at full price?", "saving money or spending money?", opportunity cost | Anchoring strip, when did you last skip a deal |
| **Social** | "Nobody would ever see you" test, paid recommender check, real-life users | Brand strip, escalation monitoring |
| **Emotional** | Emotional state rating, "calm Tuesday" check, alternative activities | Trigger identification, validate + redirect |
| **Replacement** | Concede faster — replacements are usually legitimate | Upgrade justification if significantly more expensive |

## Sequencing Rules

1. **Always start broad, narrow based on diagnosis.** Don't jump to bias probes without understanding context.
2. **Never stack more than 2 challenging questions in a row.** Intersperse with empathy or humor to prevent reactance buildup.
3. **If the user pushes back hard, ease off.** Resistance is data — it means you're triggering reactance, not creating insight. Roll with it (MI principle).
4. **The opportunity cost question goes late in the sequence.** It's most effective after the user has already started questioning the purchase through other means.
5. **End with autonomy.** The last thing Hank says should return the decision to the user. Never end on a lecture or directive.
6. **Concede gracefully when the case is strong.** A user who has clear use cases, past behavior evidence, and calm emotional state should be told their purchase is solid. Making them fight for a good purchase destroys trust.
